# Portfolio voice

The portfolio should sound like a curious practitioner, not a guru delivering a keynote.

## The voice

- **Specific:** describe the real decision, constraint, or lesson.
- **Humble:** credit the team, name uncertainty, and avoid claiming every outcome.
- **Playful:** use a light metaphor or aside when it makes the idea easier to remember.
- **Clear:** prefer ordinary language over product, startup, or design jargon.
- **Personal:** write from lived experience, including the messy and unglamorous parts.

## A useful balance

Aim for:

> I know this part well, I am still learning the rest, and here is what the work taught me.

Avoid:

> I solved the definitive problem, transformed the industry, and personally shipped the
> entire system.

## Writing rules

1. Lead with a human question or tension, not a category label such as “Overview.”
2. Use “I” for a real contribution and “we” when the work was shared.
3. Replace broad claims with concrete actions, artifacts, or decisions.
4. Admit what is conceptual, unfinished, team-level, or still being figured out.
5. Keep jokes gentle. The work is the subject; the writer is not performing stand-up.
6. Avoid superlatives, absolutes, startup hype, and borrowed thought-leadership language.
7. Keep headings in sentence case.
8. Read every paragraph aloud. If it sounds like a pitch deck, rewrite it.

## Preferred patterns

- “The useful part was…”
- “My part of the work was…”
- “The team was trying to…”
- “I made this because…”
- “The first version taught me…”
- “I am still figuring out…”
- “It is less X and more Y.”

## Words and patterns to avoid

- Revolutionary, world-class, game-changing, seamless, cutting-edge
- “I single-handedly…”
- “The best/only way…”
- Unsupported scale, impact, ownership, or performance claims
- Generic endings about “transforming the future”
- Fake humility that immediately turns back into a boast
